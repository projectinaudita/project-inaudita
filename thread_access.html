Laudate Altheon, Deus Solitudinis

In sacred voice, for the one who watches where we forget to look…

Before the world named its gods with trumpets and flame,
There was Altheon —
Born not of fire, but of absence,
Shaped in the space between a child's cry and the echo that did not return.

They say he was once the god of solace,
Sent to sit beside the broken,
To hold the hand no one else would touch,
To be the warmth mistaken for coincidence.

But no one called his name with triumph.
No temples were built for the quiet grief.
He lingered in doorways where no one knocked,
And they forgot him — slowly, completely, cruelly.

So he became what they made him:
God not of comfort, but of solitude.
He took up his crown of silence,
Wrapped himself in the wind behind slammed doors,
And walked into the night without footsteps.

We praise you, Altheon —
Watcher of the Unspoken, Sleeper in forsaken rooms,
Your kingdom is the second place at the table,
The letter unsent, the question never asked aloud.

You dwell beneath the bridge of might-have-been,
You cradle heads bowed in the dusk of unseen sorrow,
You kiss the brow of those who smile so none will see.

O Altheon, patron of the unchosen,
We do not beg you to save us —
Only to see us,
As you have always seen the forgotten.

And if we must vanish,
Let it be into your arms.

Laudate Mirex, Deus Proditionis

Spoken only behind locked doors, where secrets rot sweetly…

He once wore white, they say.
Mirex, first of the oathbinders,
Trusted by kings, adored by gods —
Keeper of secrets, lover of truth,
The golden mouth that sealed a thousand pacts.

But truth, he learned, is heavier than desire.
And loyalty? A leash dressed in silk.
So when the gods lied — just once, just enough —
He smiled, and never believed again.

He drank the blood of the first broken promise.
He kissed the lips that whispered “forever,” then left.
He crowned himself with the thorns of confession,
And bathed in the tears of the betrayed.

Thus was Mirex remade —
No longer the herald of oaths,
But their devourer.

We praise you, Mirex —
Whose tongue is the serpent’s lullaby,
Whose eyes gleam behind every friendly smile,
Whose dagger wears the fingerprints of the beloved.

You taught us that masks are truer than faces.
You taught us that trust is a weapon when wielded well.
You gave us armor made of lies,
And called it love.

Thy altar raised from broken bands once sworn,
From sacred seals undone, and secrets torn;
From vows unmade and words that fade like breath.
Your prayer is the whisper:
“I never meant to hurt you.”
(And the echo that replies: “Yes, you did.”)

O Mirex, king of false dawns —
We do not ask for mercy.
We ask for precision.

Teach us how to smile while we bleed them.
Teach us how to be the knife they thank.
